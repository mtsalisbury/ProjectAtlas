The Internet Recognizes Accounts. Atlas Recognizes People.

The Internet can move traffic across the world in milliseconds, but it still has no persistent concept of the individual. Project Atlas asks whether that missing concept has become an architectural limitation.

By Mike SalisburyPublished July 19, 20268 minute readOpen research initiative

Every day, billions of people sign in to applications, websites, cloud services, and corporate networks. We often say that we are “on the Internet,” yet the Internet itself has no durable concept of us as individuals.

It recognizes accounts.

Google knows a Google account. Microsoft knows a Microsoft account. GitHub knows a GitHub account. An employer knows an Active Directory account. A bank knows a customer record. A government knows a citizen record.

Each identity exists inside an administrative boundary. None of those systems represents the whole person, and none of them remains portable when the boundary changes.

The person remains constant. The identity systems around that person do not.

The Internet has a blind spot

The modern Internet is an ecosystem of disconnected identities. Every service creates its own representation of who someone is, forcing people to prove themselves repeatedly, manage credentials repeatedly, and rebuild trust repeatedly.

This fragmentation is so familiar that it can appear inevitable. It is not. It is the result of how the Internet and its applications evolved.

Identity is not portable

Consider what happens when someone changes jobs. Their corporate email address is disabled. VPN access is removed. Certificates are revoked. Internal permissions disappear. The digital identity associated with that organization may vanish within hours.

Yet the person has not disappeared. Their knowledge, relationships, professional history, and accountability remain. The identity system was temporary because it belonged to the employer, not to the individual.

The same pattern appears when changing universities, cloud platforms, service providers, devices, or countries. The person is rebuilt as a new collection of accounts because current systems define identity through organizational ownership.

The Internet was built around devices

This is not a design failure. It reflects the problem the Internet was originally built to solve.

Internet Protocol moves packets between hosts. Routers exchange reachability. Switches learn addresses. Firewalls enforce policy. Applications establish sessions. The architecture is excellent at connecting networks and devices.

People exist above that architecture as data managed by applications. As cloud computing, mobile access, remote work, software-as-a-service, and global collaboration expanded, the consequences became more visible: the Internet moves people between networks, but it does not move their identity with the same consistency.

A different starting point

Project Atlas does not begin by asking how to improve another login system. It asks whether the person should become a persistent architectural reference point across systems.

What if we started with the person?

Project Atlas asks a simple but fundamental question: What if the Internet recognized people instead of only accounts?

Imagine a persistent Internet presence controlled by the individual. It would not replace banks, governments, employers, identity providers, or applications. Those systems would continue to manage their own relationships and policies.

The difference is that the person would no longer need to be recreated from nothing inside every new system. A Personal Internet Presence could provide a stable point around which relationships, credentials, consent, context, and trust are formed.

Changing employers would not change who the person is. Changing devices would not require identity to begin again. Changing networks would not erase the continuity of trust.

The person becomes the constant. Everything else becomes context.

Beyond authentication

Most identity discussions focus on authentication: passwords, certificates, multi-factor authentication, passkeys, identity providers, and single sign-on.

These technologies answer an essential question: can someone prove they are authorized to use a service or claim an identity?

Atlas asks a broader architectural question: should the Internet have a persistent concept of the individual that is not owned by a single provider?

Authentication would remain important, but it would become one component of a wider model that also includes portability, relationship context, selective disclosure, trust, revocation, recovery, accountability, and privacy.

A foundation, not another platform

Project Atlas is not intended to become another social network, universal identity provider, or centralized profile service.

It is an open research initiative investigating whether existing Internet, identity, network, cloud, and security technologies can be organized around a person-controlled layer without creating a global tracking mechanism or a new concentration of power.

That requires difficult questions:

Questions the research must answer

How should trust be established? How should privacy be preserved? How should relationships be limited and revoked? How should identity recovery work? How can interoperability be achieved without centralizing control? How would abuse, coercion, correlation, and surveillance be prevented?

Why publish the research openly?

The Internet became successful because its protocols and assumptions were debated, tested, challenged, and improved in public.

Project Atlas follows the same approach. The objective is not to declare the thesis correct. The objective is to expose it to criticism from network architects, security engineers, identity professionals, privacy researchers, distributed-systems engineers, standards contributors, and anyone who can identify where the model fails.

Good ideas improve through criticism. Weak ideas should be discarded before they become infrastructure.

An invitation

Project Atlas is not presented as a finished solution. It is a conversation about the future of Internet identity and presence.

If the Internet were designed today—with decades of experience in cybersecurity, cloud computing, mobile systems, privacy, distributed architecture, and global connectivity—would identity still be rebuilt inside every application and institution?

Or would we start with the individual?
